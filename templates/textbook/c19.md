## 1 Introduction

We saw in Chapter 18 that different stakeholders have different information needs and that there are limitations on how useful the financial statements can be in isolation. Companies have therefore sought to provide more information to stakeholders in the form of **narrative reporting**, often supported by additional performance measures.

![Stakeholder Perspective](media/images/StakeholderPerspective.png)

Stakeholder perspective

Investors and creditors need information so they can assess the **future prospects** of the entity. To help investors and creditors make this assessment, narrative reporting should not only **explain the reasons** for the past performance of the business but should be **forward-looking** and should explain the entity’s strategy, risks and opportunities, and plans for the future. **Sustainability issues** can create both **risks and opportunities** for an entity. Investors and creditors therefore need information about these sustainability-related risks and opportunities in order to make informed assessments of the entity’s future prospects. Investors, creditors and wider stakeholders increasingly also want to know whether the **entity’s business practices are environmentally and socially responsible,** and whether they **contribute to sustainable development**. Companies are responding to these information needs by **disclosing sustainability-related information**, either in their annual reports or in separate **sustainability reports**.

In this chapter, we will focus our attention firstly on sustainability, **sustainability reporting** and the **IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards**. We will then cover **management commentary** and the IASB’s guidance on producing management commentary that is useful to investors.

## 2 Sustainability reporting

Sustainability reporting is now a **key part of a company's dialogue with its stakeholders.** In fact, the stakeholder desire for and expectation of such information is so strong that companies which fail to provide sustainability disclosures will likely now be at a significant disadvantage in terms of attracting and retaining investors and appealing to wider stakeholders.

Sustainability reporting is therefore currently the biggest focus in corporate reporting. However, it has been difficult to navigate, for both preparers and stakeholders, due to the many different sources of guidance and regulatory requirements across the world. The IFRS Foundation has sought to address this issue through the formation of the **International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB)** and the issue of **IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards**.

In this section, we will consider first the concepts of sustainability and of impacts and dependencies, before looking at some existing reporting frameworks for sustainability. In the next section we will look at the IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards in detail.

### 2.1 What is 'sustainability'?

Sustainability is not a new idea. But it is a concept that has gained significant global attention in more recent years. Now sustainability and sustainable development have become a central focus of international discussions and policies.

Sustainable development was defined by the United Nations Brundtland Commission as development that ‘meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs’ (*UN, 1987*). The report of the Brundtland Commission emphasised the **interconnectedness** **of** **environmental, social and economic issues** in sustainable development.

It is important to recognise that sustainability is not just about environmental issues. Sustainability is generally recognised to encompass issues of:

- Environment
- Society
- Economics
- Governance

As sustainability continues to be a key global issue, there is **increasing pressure** on entities from investors and other stakeholders to both **conduct their operations in a sustainable way** and **to report** on how they are doing this.

#### 2.1.1 The Sustainable Development Goals

In 2015, the United Nations member states pledged commitment to the *2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development* as ‘a plan of action for people, planet and prosperity’ *(UN, 2015)*. At the heart of the agenda are the 17 **Sustainable Development Goals** (SDGs), which include:

- **No Poverty**: End poverty in all its forms everywhere.
- **Decent Work and Economic Growth**: Promote sustained, inclusive, and sustainable economic growth, full and productive employment, and decent work for all.
- **Reduced Inequality**: Reduce inequality within and among countries.
- **Climate Action**: Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts.
- **Life Below Water**: Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas, and marine resources for sustainable development.
- **Life on Land**: Protect, restore, and promote sustainable use of terrestrial ecosystems, sustainably manage forests, combat desertification, halt and reverse land degradation, and halt biodiversity loss.

*(UN, 2015)*

The SDGs set the priorities for governments and apply to all nations. To meet the goals, it will require effort from all aspects of society, including businesses.

### 2.2 Impacts and dependencies

There are two fundamental aspects of sustainability as it relates to business: **impacts** and **dependencies**.

| Impacts | ‘Impacts’ refers to the effect that an entity has on its stakeholders, society, the economy and the natural environment, through the resources it uses and the relationships it has in its value chain.For example: worker rights, human rights, waste, greenhouse gas emissions, water use |
| --- | --- |
| Dependencies | ‘Dependencies’ refers to the reliance an entity has on its stakeholders, society, the economy and the natural environment through the resources it uses and the relationships it has in its value chain.For example: worker health, diversity, climate risks, resource availability, consumer expectations, regulatory risks |

A ‘value chain’ is defined by the ISSB as ‘the full range of interactions, resources and relationships related to a reporting entity’s business model and the external environment in which it operates’ (*IFRS S1: Appendix A*).

Information on **dependencies** is typically **more useful for investors, lenders and creditors** as dependencies create **risks and opportunities** which can affect the **entity’s prospects**.

Information on **impacts** is typically **more useful for broader stakeholders**, such as governments, consumers, employees and wider society who want to know how the entity’s actions and future strategy will impact both people and planet.

However, it is of course more nuanced than this, as investors, lenders and creditors are increasingly interested in whether the companies they provide capital to are **socially and environmentally responsible**, and an entity’s impacts may therefore form part of the investing or lending decision.

Additionally, an **entity’s impacts may also create risks and opportunities that affect the entity’s prospects**, and information on these impacts is therefore useful to investors, lenders and creditors. For example, an entity may have a poor record in terms of worker rights or pollution, both of which are impacts of its actions, but these impacts could result in risk to the entity’s reputation, which would ultimately affect its future prospects.

### 2.3 ESG vs Sustainability

The language surrounding sustainability issues can be confusing as there is not one source of globally understood definitions. Often the terms ‘ESG’ (Environmental, Social and Governance) and ’sustainability’ are used interchangeably, however, it is generally understood that **ESG is a sub-category of sustainability**, considering risks and opportunities related to the environment, society and governance issues and how these can affect enterprise value. ESG tends therefore to be more about an entity’s dependencies than its impacts and so is more useful for investors and creditors than broader stakeholders. Sustainability is a broader concept that includes a company's commitment to responsible business practices and is relevant to a wider range of stakeholders.

### 2.4 Reporting frameworks

The increased global interest in sustainability and the demand for transparency from investors on sustainability-related issues resulted in the emergence of different regulatory and voluntary sustainability disclosure frameworks. Each framework had a slightly different focus: some focussed mainly on an entity’s impacts whereas others focussed mainly on the risks and opportunities arising from the entity’s impacts and dependencies.

The many different sources of guidance and regulatory requirements that emerged caused difficulties for preparers who were not sure which guidance to apply, and for investors, who reported inconsistency between entities and variation in the quality and quantity of information provided. The IFRS Foundation sought to address this with the formation of the **International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB)** and the creation of a comprehensive global set of sustainability standards that are focussed on meeting the information needs of investors.

As part of this process, the **Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB)**, the **Climate Disclosure Standards Board (CDSB)** and the **International Integrated Reporting Council** were consolidated into the IFRS Foundation. The principles in the guidance issued by these organisations, and the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) (section 2.4.2), were used by the ISSB to develop the **IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards** (covered in section 3).

#### 2.4.1 Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)

The GRI Standards are currently the most widely used set of sustainability reporting standards in the world (*GRI, no date*).

The objective of sustainability reporting using the GRI Standards is to provide transparency on how an entity **contributes or aims to contribute to sustainable development** (GRI, 2021). The GRI standards focus on reporting the **entity’s impacts on the economy, the environment and people** and how the entity manages these impacts. A principle of the GRI standards is stakeholder inclusiveness and requires businesses to identify **stakeholders beyond investors and lenders**.

The focus of the GRI Standards is on reporting an **entity’s impacts, and not its dependencies**. Therefore, it is limited in its usefulness to investors when they are assessing the future prospects of an entity.

#### 2.4.2 Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD)

The TCFD was established by the Financial Stability Board (FSB) to improve and increase the reporting of climate-related financial information. The recommendations of the TCFD are **investor- and lender-focused.**

The recommendations require entities to disclose the **financial impact** of **climate-related risks and opportunities**, and to assess the **resilience of the business strategy** to future climate outcomes. The recommendations therefore require **forward-looking financial disclosure.**

The recommendations are structured around disclosure of four core elements (*TCFD, 2021*):

- **Governance**: the entity’s governance around climate-related risks and opportunities
- **Strategy**: the actual and potential impacts of climate-related risks and opportunities on the entity, where material
- **Risk**: how the entity identifies, assesses and manages climate-related risks
- **Metrics and targets:** the metrics and targets used to assess and manage relevant climate-related risks and opportunities, where material

**Scenario analysis** is recommended so that entities can explore future business risks arising from climate change. The findings of scenario analysis should be presented in the narrative report.

The recommendations of the TCFD are currently mandatory for some entities in some jurisdictions, for example, in the UK and in Japan.

The ISSB used the TCFD recommendations to develop IFRS S2 *Climate-related Disclosures*. In October 2023, the TCFD was disbanded, having met its remit, and the FSB asked the IFRS Foundation to take over the monitoring of the progress of companies’ climate-related disclosures.

#### 2.4.3 Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD)

The TNFD was established with the aim to provide better quality information in corporate reporting on **nature-related issues**. In September 2023, the TNFD released its **voluntary recommendations** and guidance. The recommendations are designed to help entities ‘both report on and act on evolving nature-related issues with the ultimate aim of supporting a shift in global financial flows away from nature-negative outcomes and toward nature-positive outcomes’ (*TNFD, no date(a)*).

The TNFD was borne out of the recognition that all of society, including business, depends on nature’s assets and the services they provide. But that 'nature is deteriorating globally and biodiversity is declining faster than at any time in human history. The majority of the vital ecosystem services on which business and society depend, and which provide the foundation for every economy, are in decline’ (*TNFD, no date(b)*).

A business has both dependencies on nature and impacts on nature. These dependencies and impacts create nature-related risks and opportunities. The recommendations of the TNFD create a framework for entities to identify, manage and disclose, where appropriate, their dependencies and impacts, risks and opportunities to provide useful information for investors and other stakeholders.

The recommendations of the TNFD are structured around the same core elements as the TCFD: **governance, strategy, risk management, metrics and targets**.

#### 2.4.4 European Union’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

The CSRD, which seeks to significantly expand the reporting on sustainability by companies in order to help stakeholders, was adopted by the EU in 2022. The first reporting under the CSRD, using the newly issued European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRSs), will relate to accounting periods commencing 1 January 2024, with a phased implementation for certain companies over three years. ESRS 1 *General Requirements* requires certain sustainability information to be included in a company’s annual report, in a dedicated section of the management report. The structure of the reporting is specified in ESRS 1. The sustainability information must be subject to external assurance.

Under the ESRSs, companies must report on four areas: governance; strategy; impact, risk and opportunity management; and metrics and targets, over three topics:

- Environment – encompassing climate change, pollution, water and marine resources, biodiversity and ecosystems, resource use and circular economy
- Social – encompassing own workforce, workers in the value chain, affected communities, consumers and end users
- Governance – business conduct

*(KPMG, 2023)*

The ESRSs specify some mandatory disclosures, which must be made by all entities, and other disclosures which are only required if they are material.

The concept of **double materiality** is central to the ESRSs as an entity must undertake a **double materiality assessment** as the starting point to its sustainability reporting under ESRSs.

> **Key terms**
>
> **Double materiality:**
> Considering both **impact materiality** and **financial materiality** from a sustainability perspective. **Impact materiality** considers whether a company’s activities (direct or indirect) significantly impact on people and planet. **Financial materiality** considers the risks and opportunities arising from sustainability issues which may have a material impact on the company’s value in the short, medium or long term.
>

A sustainability matter is material if it meets either the criteria for impact materiality or for financial materiality.

The ESRSs therefore require disclosure of both an entity’s impacts as well as the risks and opportunities arising from its impacts and dependencies. However, as this reporting is yet to be applied in practice, it is not clear how entities will interpret the requirements – for example, could the requirement to report on impacts be interpreted as only the impacts that may affect the entity’s future prospects?

The development of the ESRSs included co-operation with the ISSB and the GRI to try and increase interoperability between the standards. How successful this has been with regards to the ISSB Standards is not yet clear given that they use different definitions of materiality (see section 3.2.2).

#### Activity 1: Benefits of sustainability reporting

What are some of the potential benefits **to the company** of preparing a sustainability report?

**Solution**

There are many potential benefits to the company of preparing a sustainability report. Some of the benefits will be internal to the company and some external.

Internal benefits include:

- Gaining an increased understanding of the sustainability-related risks and opportunities facing the business, thereby allowing for responses to mitigate the risks and to take advantage of the opportunities.
- The ability to benchmark and assess performance in relation to sustainability issues, with respect to laws, norms, codes and voluntary initiatives.
- Enhanced ability to comply with laws and regulations by adopting systems that allow for reporting on sustainability-related issues
- Reducing the risk of being implicated in publicised environmental, social and governance failures by being aware of the risks facing the business and compliance with laws and regulations

External benefits include:

- Increased understanding of the company’s impacts on the environment and society allows the company to better plan how to lessen or reverse those impacts and instead strive for positive impacts, so contributing to sustainable development
- Instilling confidence in investors through transparency of the risks and opportunities faced and how the business will mitigate and capitalise on these to deliver long-term growth
- Improved stakeholder engagement, involving stakeholders in the reporting process and allowing them to better understand the company’s values and ability to create value
- Potential to improve reputation and build trust through commitment to transparency

## 3 IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards

IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards are issued by the International Sustainability Standards Board. IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards are also known as ‘ISSB Standards’.

### 3.1 International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB)

The ISSB was formed in 2021 and comprises members from a range of professional backgrounds and geographical areas with experience and expertise relevant to sustainability.

The ISSB has four key objectives:

1. To develop standards for a global baseline of sustainability disclosures;
2. To meet the information needs of investors;
3. To enable companies to provide comprehensive sustainability information to global capital markets; and
4. To facilitate interoperability with disclosures that are jurisdiction-specific and/or aimed at broader stakeholder groups.

*(IFRS Foundation, n.d.)*

![Stakeholder Perspective](media/images/StakeholderPerspective.png)

Stakeholder perspective

Notice that the objectives of the ISSB focus on the information needs of investors and on providing information to capital markets. This is likely to mean that the impacts of the entity are not fully reported and may be limited to the extent that they affect investors and the capital markets. This is a narrower scope than the requirements of the EU CSRD and an entirely different scope to the GRI Standards. The ISSB has never shied away from the fact that its standards are to satisfy investors, as evidenced by the fourth objective around interoperability with other disclosures which are aimed at broader stakeholder groups. However, it is debatable whether this will be understood by wider stakeholders when they are viewing the annual reports of companies.

The ISSB issued the first two IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards in June 2023:

- IFRS S1 *General Requirem**ents for Disclosure of Sustainability-related Financial Information*
- IFRS S2 *Climate-related Disclosures*

IFRS S1 sets out the general requirements for disclosing sustainability-related information. IFRS S1 refers companies to other ISSB Standards or to other sources for detailed requirements on different topics. For climate-related disclosures, entities should apply IFRS S2. As there are currently no other issued ISSB Standards, an entity should use other sources (primarily the SASB Standards) for guidance on what to disclose on other sustainability-related topics.

An entity must apply both standards in order to comply with IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards.

The standards are effective for reporting periods beginning from 1 January 2024, though earlier adoption is permitted provided both standards are applied together.

The ISSB Standards can be accessed on the IFRS Foundation website here: https://www.ifrs.org/issued-standards/ifrs-sustainability-standards-navigator/

### 3.2 IFRS S1 *General Requirements for Disclosure of Sustainability-related Financial Information*

#### 3.2.1 Objectives and scope

The objective of IFRS S1 is to require an entity to disclose information about its **sustainability-related risks and opportunities** that is useful to investors when making decisions relating to providing resources to the entity (*IFRS S1: para. 1*).

IFRS S1 is built on the concept that an **entity’s prospects** (ie its ability to generate cash flows, its access to finance or its cost of capital over the short, medium or long term) are **inextricably linked** to how the entity interacts with its **stakeholders** and with **society** (relationships), and with the **economy** and the **natural environment** (resources) (*IFRS S1: paras. 2–3*).

Sustainability-related risks and opportunities arise through an entity’s **dependencies** on those relationships and resources, and its **impacts** upon those relationships and resources.

IFRS S1 requires disclosure about all sustainability-related risks and opportunities that could **reasonably be expected to affect the entity’s prospects**. This includes those that arise in the entity’s value chain.

An entity can apply the ISSB Standards whether it also applies IFRS Accounting Standards or another GAAP in its financial statements.

IFRS S1 specifies that an entity’s sustainability information should be provided as part of its annual report, along with its financial statements (*IFRS S1: para. 60*).

#### 3.2.2 Conceptual foundations

The conceptual foundations of IFRS S1 are built on the concepts in the IASB’s *Conceptual Framework for Financial Reporting.*

Information on sustainability-related risks and opportunities must be (*IFRS S1: paras. 1–9*):

- **Presented fairly**
- Useful, which means **relevant** and a **faithful representation**. The usefulness is enhanced if the information is comparable, verifiable, timely and understandable.

Relevance includes the concept of materiality, which is aligned with the definition used in IAS 1 to facilitate connectivity across the entity’s financial report.

> **Key terms**
>
> **Material sustainability-related financial information:**
> In the context of sustainability-related financial disclosures, information is material if omitting, misstating or obscuring that information could reasonably be expected to influence decisions that primary users of general-purpose financial reports make on the basis of those reports, which include financial statements and sustainability-related financial disclosures and which provide information about a specific reporting entity. *(IFRS S1: para. 18)*
>

IFRS S1 also specifies that:

- The **reporting entity** should be the same as the reporting entity in the financial statements (*IFRS S1: para. 20*).
- The information provided should enable investors to understand the **connections** between it, for example between different risks and opportunities or to the financial statements (*IFRS S1: para. 21*).

#### 3.2.3 Core content

IFRS S1 requires a company to disclose how it manages its sustainability-related risks and opportunities under four headings: **governance**, **strategy**, **risk management**, and **metrics and targets** (*IFRS S1: paras. 25–53*). This core content is built on (and includes) the requirements of the TCFD recommendations (see section 2.4.2) but extended to cover issues **beyond climate**.

**Governance**

| Disclosures about (para. 25): | Including (para. 27): |
| --- | --- |
| The governance processes, controls and procedures used by the entity to monitor and manage sustainability-related risks and opportunities | - Who is responsible for sustainability at a governance level and how that responsibility is worked out (detailed disclosure requirements are given)<br>- Management’s role in the governance processes, controls and procedures to monitor and oversee sustainability-related risks and opportunities |

| Disclosures about (para. 25): | Including (para. 29): |

| Disclosures about (para. 25): | Including: |

#### 3.2.4 General requirements

To **identify** the company’s sustainability-related risks and opportunities, an entity should apply the **relevant ISSB Standard**. If there is no relevant standard (as is currently the case for all sector-specific disclosures and all topics except climate), then the appropriate **SASB Standard** should be used. IFRS S1 also points to other sources, such as the CDSB framework, other standard setting bodies and the risks and opportunities identified by other entities operating in the same industry (*IFRS S1: paras. 54–59*).

The IFRS Foundation has taken over responsibility for the SASB Standards with the intention to maintain them as necessary.

### 3.3 IFRS S2 *Climate-related Disclosures*

#### 3.3.1 Scope and objectives

The objective of IFRS S2 is to require an entity to disclose information about **climate-related risks and opportunities** that are reasonably expected to affect the entity’s prospects (*IFRS S2: paras. 1–2*). IFRS 2 incorporates and builds on the recommendations of the TCFD, meaning much of its content follows the structure of the TCFD recommendations.

Climate-related risks and opportunities arise from an entity’s **impacts and dependencies** on people and the environment (*IFRS S2: para. BC26*):

- Dependencies, for example changes in the availability or quality of a resource such as water, can lead to climate-related risks for the entity.
- An entity’s impacts can result in climate-related risks and opportunities if the impact affects the resources or relationships an entity depends on. For example, greenhouse gas emissions (impact) could result in a climate-related risk if there is an expected shift in consumer preference to carbon-neutral alternatives.

*(IFRS S2: para. BC27–BC28)*

IFRS 2 therefore requires disclosure of impacts and dependencies in so far as that information is material (*IFRS S2: para. BC29*).

![Stakeholder Perspective](media/images/StakeholderPerspective.png)

Stakeholder perspective

In the ISSB Standards, sustainability-related information is ‘material’ if it could reasonably be expected to influence decisions made by primary users. The focus of IFRS S2 is on primary users – investors, lenders and creditors – and providing information to satisfy their needs. So while IFRS S2 requires disclosure of impacts, and does include a significant amount of disclosure about greenhouse gas emissions (see section 3.3.2), this disclosure is apparently limited to impacts that might affect the prospects of the entity. It is therefore narrower in scope than disclosure that would be required under GRI Standards or the EU CSRD. While it still may be useful to other stakeholders, such as consumers, it is unlikely to present the full picture of an entity’s impact on climate.

IFRS S2 divides climate-related risks into two major categories: **transition risks** and **physical risks** (*IFRS S2: paras. 3–4*).

> **Key terms**
>
> **Climate-related transition risks:**
> Risks that arise from efforts to transition to a lower-carbon economy. Transition risks include **policy, legal, technological, market** and **reputational risks**. These risks could carry financial implications for an entity, such as increased operating costs or asset impairment due to new or amended climate-related regulations. The entity's financial performance could also be affected by shifting consumer demands and the development and deployment of new technology. *(IFRS S2: Appendix A)*
>
> **Climate-related physical risks:**
> Risks resulting from climate change that can be event-driven (acute physical risk) or from longer-term shifts in climatic patterns (chronic physical risk). Acute physical risks arise from weather-related events such as storms, floods, drought. Chronic physical risks arise from longer-term shifts in climate which could lead to sea level rise, biodiversity loss etc.These risks could carry financial implications for an entity, such as costs resulting from direct damage to assets or indirect effects of supply-chain disruption. The entity's financial performance could also be affected by changes in water availability, sourcing and quality; and extreme temperature changes affecting the entity's premises, operations, supply chains, transportation needs and employee health and safety. *(IFRS S2: Appendix A)*
>

IFRS S2 requires disclosure of **material climate-related opportunities**, as well as risks. Climate related opportunities ‘refers to the potential positive effects arising from climate change for an entity. Efforts to mitigate and adapt to climate change can produce climate-related opportunities for an entity’. *(IFRS S2: Appendix A)*

#### 3.3.2 Core content

The core content of IFRS S2 follows the four areas of governance, strategy, risk management and metrics and targets seen in IFRS S1.

**Governance and risk management**

The requirements in IFRS S2 for governance and risk management are very similar to those in IFRS S1 for general sustainability-related risks and opportunities, other than IFRS S2 references ‘**climate-related** risks and opportunities’.

If sustainability-related risks and opportunities are managed on an integrated basis, then an entity can provide integrated disclosures and should avoid duplication.

**Strategy**

For strategy, many of the requirements are similar to IFRS S1, however, there are a significant amount of additional climate-specific disclosures that are required, including:

- Information on an entity’s **climate-related transition plan**, which is defined below (para. 9)
- Information on whether risks are **physical risks** or **transition risks** (para. 10)
- How the entity plans to achieve any climate-related targets it has set and any targets it is required to meet by law or regulation (para. 14)
- Climate related risks and opportunities for which there is a significant risk of a material adjustment to assets/liabilities in the next reporting period (para. 15)
- Climate resilience, which is the resilience of the entity to climate-related changes. **Scenario analysis** is required by IFRS S2 (para. 22)

IFRS S2 contains industry-specific guidance to help identify climate-related risks and opportunities.

> **Key terms**
>
> **Climate-related transition plan:**
> An aspect of an entity’s overall strategy that lays out the entity’s targets, actions or resources for its transition towards a lower-carbon economy, including actions such as reducing its greenhouse gas emissions. *(IFRS S2: Appendix A)*
>

**Metrics and targets**

Information disclosed should enable primary users to understand the entity’s performance in relation to climate-related risks and opportunities, including progress towards climate-related targets it has set and those it is required to meet by law or regulation.

IFRS S2 requires all entities to disclose metrics related to (para. 29):

1. **Greenhouse gas (GHG)** - specifically Scope 1, 2 and 3 GHG emissions measured in accordance with the Greenhouse Gas Protocol (unless required to use a different measure by law/regulation) and subject to specific disclosure requirements
2. **Climate-related transition risks** – the amount and percentage of assets or business activities vulnerable to these risks
3. **Climate-related physical risks** – the amount and percentage of assets or business activities vulnerable to these risks
4. **Climate-related opportunities** – the amount and percentage of assets or business activities aligned with climate-related opportunities
5. **Capital deployment** – the amount of capital expenditure, financing or investment deployed towards climate-related risks and opportunities
6. **Internal carbon prices** (*a mechanism for an entity to put a value on GHG emissions so they can be considered in decision making. Can help drive reduction in emissions*) - whether and how internal carbon pricing is used in decision making and the price per tonne of GHG emissions that the entity has used
7. **Remuneration** - whether and how climate considerations are factored into remuneration of directors

In terms of **targets**, IFRS S2 requires disclosure of quantitative and qualitative climate-related targets the entity has set to monitor progress towards achieving its strategic goals, and any targets it is required to meet by law, including any GHG emissions targets (para 33).

The entity must then disclose detail about the target, including its objective, the period of time it covers, the point from which progress is measured and how the latest international agreement on climate change has informed the target (para. 33). All of these disclosures will help to make the targets **more transparent** for investors.

There are also **industry-specific disclosures** relating to metrics and targets that should be made if relevant to the entity.

![Stakeholder Perspective](media/images/StakeholderPerspective.png)

Stakeholder perspective

The SBR syllabus requires you to be able to ‘assess the usefulness of corporate disclosures of climate-related risks and opportunities’ in the context of IFRS S2. To consider this, we need to think about ‘who’ the information is useful to and what makes it useful, or not.IFRS S2 disclosures are aimed at investors, lenders and creditors. They need information to allow them to assess the prospects of an entity so that they can make informed decisions as to the provision of capital to the entity. There is generally accepted global recognition that we are in a climate crisis and that crisis creates risks and opportunities for businesses. These climate-related risks and opportunities are important to investors, lenders and creditors as they can affect their assessment of the prospects of the entity. To be useful, the disclosure must be relevant, so the information must have predictive and confirmatory value and must be material, and it must faithfully represent what it purports to represent. It therefore must be complete, neutral and free from bias. These are the fundamental principles on which the ISSB Standards are based. If an entity has applied IFRS S2, it should result in information which is useful, provided the entity has applied the standard correctly and with honesty. Investors often complain of lack of comparability in climate-related disclosure. This is an issue which can be addressed with the widespread application of IFRS S2, and through the decision of the ISSB to specify particular defined metrics, eg scope 1, 2 and 3 GHG emissions, as required. However, given IFRS S2 is not (yet) mandatory in most jurisdictions, it may take some time for the consistency in reporting to be seen. Inconsistency between the numbers and the narrative is another complaint from investors. For example, how do targets set affect the financial statements? This is something IFRS S2 goes someway to address with the transparency required around targets. IFRS S1 also requires that assumptions and data used in sustainability reporting is the same as far as possible as that used in the financial statements.Issues of climate are subject to uncertainty as we cannot predict the future with 100% accuracy. The high level of judgement and uncertainty involved in, for example, climate scenario analysis, can make information less useful to users. IFRS S2 requires significant disclosures in this area to address this issue. Returning to ‘who’ is the information useful to. Information prepared in accordance with IFRS S2 is aimed at investors and presents impacts only in so far as they create risks and opportunities for the entity. Therefore, the information could be less useful to other stakeholders who want to know how the entity’s operations impact on climate.

### 3.4 Global response

The ISSB Standards appear to have been generally well received. The fact that the standards are based on existing standards (SASB, CDSB and TCFD) and that they have been designed for interoperability with standards such as the GRI standards, means less work for businesses which already have reporting in place and perhaps less difficulty for investors in understanding the resulting reporting (*Harvard Law School, 2023*). The ISSB Standards have been endorsed by IOSCO and several countries have indicated that they plan to make reporting under the ISSB Standards mandatory, including the UK, Japan and Canada.

However, there has been some criticism of the ISSB Standards in that, unlike the EU’s CSRD, they do not consider **double materiality** (*Oxford Business Law Blog, 2023*). This means that the entity’s impacts on the environment and on society will not be disclosed, unless the entity assesses those impacts will affect the future prospects of the entity and are material to investors. One response to this is that the GRI Standards, which do report an entity’s impacts, can be used alongside the ISSB Standards.

Other criticisms levelled at the Standards include the fact that there is no requirement for external verification of the disclosures given, unlike the EU’s CSRD.

## 4 Management commentary

A ‘management commentary’ is a report prepared by management and provided alongside the financial statements. It seeks to provide a **context for interpreting a company's financial statements** by providing managements’ views on factors that have affected historical performance, and also on the factors which could affect the future prospects of the entity. It can be known by different names such as ‘operating and financial review’, ‘management’s discussion and analysis’ or ‘strategic report’.

A management commentary is not a requirement of IFRS Accounting Standards and is not needed to state compliance with IFRS Accounting Standards in the financial statements. However, in some countries, the provision of some form of management commentary is a legal requirement.

In the UK, for example, all companies (with limited exceptions) are required by the law to provide a **strategic report**, which must provide context for the financial statements, insight on the business model, objectives and strategy, principal risks and how they might affect future prospects and relevant sustainability information.

### 4.1 IFRS Practice Statement 1: *Management Commentary*

The IASB issued IFRS Practice Statement 1: *Management Commentary* in 2010 with the objective of helping management to produce a management commentary that is **useful** to the **primary users** of financial statements in their decision-making.

It is not an IFRS Accounting Standard and its application is voluntary.

#### 4.1.1 Principles

IFRS Practice Statement 1 lays out principles which, when followed by management in the preparation of a management commentary, will help to make the information as useful as possible to primary users.

A management commentary should (*IFRS Practice Statement 1: paras. 12–21*):

- Provide **management’s view** – what is important to management in managing the business?
- **Supplement** and **complement** the information in the financial statements – provide explanations of amounts in the financial statements, and additional information that is important to management but is not included in the financial statements.
- Include **forward-looking** information – including managements’ objectives and strategies to achieve them, as well as trends or uncertainties that could affect the entity and why.
- Include information which possesses the **qualitative characteristics** of the *Conceptual Framework*, so should be relevant and a faithful representation, and should be timely, verifiable, comparable and understandable.
- Include information which helps users **assess the performance of the entity** and **the actions of management** in achieving its objectives. This will help users to understand the entity’s **risks** and strategies to manage risks.

#### 4.1.2 Problems with IFRS Practice Statement 1

The IASB has conducted research and identified that management commentaries do not always provide the information that primary users need. In addition, the information needs of primary users are evolving, with more focus on sustainability issues now than ever before. Some of the issues identified by the IASB include:

- Information is too generic and too focussed on the short term
- Not enough information is given on sustainability issues affecting the entity
- Not enough information is given about the entity’s intangible resources
- Information provided is fragmented or difficult to reconcile to financial statements
- Information is difficult to compare with previous periods or with other entities
- Information provided is incomplete or unbalanced, for example focussing on the positive aspects of performance

*(ED: Management Commentary: para. IN8)*

In 2021, the IASB issued proposals for a revised IFRS Practice Statement on management commentary which sought to address these issues. Feedback on this project was discussed by the IASB in 2022, however, no further decisions on issuing the revised practice statement were made. Instead, in 2023, the ISSB sought feedback on a potential project on **integration in reporting**, conducted jointly with the IASB, which would build on the concepts in the proposals for the revised management commentary. Furthermore, the IASB sought information on the similarities and differences between the proposals for the revised management commentary and the Integrated Reporting Framework.

This is a fast-developing area. Just as the demands for information from investors are evolving, so too then are the responses from the IFRS Foundation. It is recommended that you keep up to date with developments in this area by reading relevant articles on the ACCA website.

### Knowledge diagnostic

#### 1.

#### Sustainability reporting

Sustainability encompasses issues related to environment, society, economics and governance. There are two fundamental aspects of sustainability as it relates to business: impacts and dependencies. Information on dependencies is typically more useful to investors and creditors as they result in risks and opportunities which may affect the prospects of the entity. Information on impacts is typically more useful to broader stakeholders.

Sustainability reporting is key part of a company's dialogue with its stakeholders. There is an expectation from investors that companies will make disclosures on sustainability issues.

Many sources of guidance are available for sustainability reporting, including the GRI Standards (impact focussed), TCFD (investor-focused climate disclosure) and the EU’s CSRD (uses double materiality). The IFRS Foundation formed the ISSB to create a global baseline of sustainability reporting for investors and consolidated several existing bodies (the SASB, CDSB and the Integrated Reporting Council) into itself in the process.

#### 2.

#### IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards

The first two IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards were issued in June 2023:

- IFRS S1 sets out the general requirements for disclosing sustainability-related information. It requires entities to disclose information about all sustainability-related risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity’s prospects. Information should be presented fairly and be relevant and a faithful representation. Disclosures are required around governance, strategy, risk management and metrics and targets. IFRS S1 refers to other ISSB Standards and SASB Standards for specific sustainability-related issues, such as climate.
- IFRS S2 is about climate-related disclosures and requires an entity to disclose information about climate-related risks and opportunities that are reasonably expected to affect the entity’s prospects. Risks are divided into transition risks and physical risks. Disclosures are required around governance, strategy, risk management and metrics and targets. Metrics and targets includes specific requirements around scope 1, 2 and 3 greenhouse gas emissions.

#### 3.

#### Management commentary

The purpose of the management commentary is to provide a context for interpreting a company’s financial position, performance and cash flows. It supplements and complements the financial statements and provides management’s view of performance, position and progress.
