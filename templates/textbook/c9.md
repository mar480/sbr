## 1 Lessee accounting

### 1.1 Introduction

IFRS 16 *Leases* requires lessees and lessors to provide relevant information in a manner that faithfully represents those transactions.

![Link To](media/images/LinkTo.png)

Link to the *Conceptual Framework*

The accounting treatment in the lessee's books is driven by the *Conceptual Framework*'s definitions of assets and liabilities rather than the legal form of the lease. The legal form of a lease is that the title to the underlying asset remains with the lessor during the period of the lease.

![Stakeholder Perspective](media/images/StakeholderPerspective.png)

Stakeholder perspective

Companies generally use leasing arrangements as a means of obtaining assets. Consequently, IFRS 16 requires the majority of leased assets and the associated obligations to be recognised in the financial statements. This is a significant change from the previous standard, IAS 17 *Leases*, which was criticised for allowing off balance sheet financing. While IFRS 16 has benefits for the users of financial statements in terms of transparency and comparability, it has had a significant impact on the most commonly used financial ratios, such as:

- Gearing, because debt has increased
- Asset turnover, because assets have increased
- Profit margin ratios, because rent expenses are removed and replaced with depreciation and finance costs.

This in turn affects the way in which users interpret and analyse the financial statements. For example, banks often impose loan covenants when making loans to companies. These covenants may need renegotiating if applying IFRS 16 causes a company's liabilities to increase significantly.

### 1.2 Definitions

> **Key terms**
>
> **Lease:**
> A **contract**, or part of a contract, that conveys the **right to use** an asset (the underlying asset) for a period of time **in exchange for consideration**. *(IFRS 16: Appendix A)*
>

A lease arises where the customer obtains the right to use the **asset**. Where it is the supplier that controls the asset used, a service rather than a lease arises.

#### 1.2.1 Identifying a lease

An entity must identify whether a contract contains a lease, which is the case if the contract conveys the **right to control** the use of an **identified asset** for a period of time in exchange for consideration (*IFRS 16: para. 9*).

The **right to control** an asset arises where, throughout the period of use, the customer has (*IFRS 16: para. B9*):

1. The right to obtain **substantially all of the economic benefits** from use of the identified asset; and
2. The **right to direct the use** of the identified asset.

The **identified asset** is typically explicitly specified in a contract. However, an asset can also be identified by being implicitly specified at the time that the asset is made available for use by the customer (*IFRS 16: para. B13*).

Even if an asset is specified, a customer **does not have the right** to use an identified asset if the **supplier has the substantive right to substitute** the asset throughout the period of use (*IFRS 16: para. B14*).

Where a contract contains **multiple components**, the consideration is allocated to each lease and non-lease component based on relative stand-alone prices (the price the lessor or similar supplier would charge for the component, or a similar component, separately) (*IFRS 16: paras. 13–14*).

#### Illustration 1: Identifying a lease

Under a four-year agreement a car seat wholesaler (WH) buys its seats from a manufacturer (MF).

Under the terms of the agreement, WH licenses its know-how to MF royalty-free to allow it to construct a machine capable of manufacturing the car seats to WH's specifications. Ownership of the know-how remains with WH and the machine has an economic life of four years.

WH pays an amount per car seat produced to MF; however, the agreement states that a minimum payment will be guaranteed each year to allow MF to recover the cost of its investment in the machinery.

The agreement states that the machinery cannot be used to make seats for other customers of MF and that WH can purchase the machinery at any time (at a price equivalent to the minimum guaranteed payments not yet paid).

**Required**

How should WH account for this arrangement?

**Solution**

The agreement is a contract containing a lease component (for the use of the machinery, the 'identified asset' in the contract) and a non-lease component (the purchase of inventories).

WH will obtain substantially all of the economic benefits from the use of the machinery over the period of the agreement as it will be able to sell on all the car seat output for its own cash flow benefit, and has the right to direct its use, as it cannot be used to make seats for other customers.

The payments that WH makes will need to be split into amounts covering the purchase of car seat inventories, and amounts which represent lease payments for use of the machine. The allocation will be based on relative stand-alone prices for hiring the machine and buying the inventories (or for a similar machine and inventories).

#### 1.2.2 Lease term

> **Key terms**
>
> **Lease term:**
> 'The **non-cancellable period** for which a lessee has the **right to use** an underlying **asset**, together with both:
>
> 1. Periods covered by an **option to extend** the lease if the lessee is **reasonably certain to exercise** that option; and
> 2. Periods covered by an **option to terminate** the lease if the lessee is **reasonably certain not to exercise** that option.' *(IFRS 16: Appendix A)*
>

The lease term is relevant when determining the period over which a leased asset should be depreciated (see below).

Example: Lease term

A lease contract is for five years with lease payments of $10,000 per annum. The lease contract contains a clause which allows the lessee to extend the lease for a further period of three years for a lease payment of $5 per annum (as it is unlikely the lessor would be able to lease the asset to another party). The economic life of the asset is estimated to be approximately eight years.

The lessee assesses it is highly likely the lease extension would be taken. The lease term is therefore eight years.

### 1.3 Accounting treatment

#### 1.3.1 Recognition

At the commencement date (the date the lessor makes the underlying asset available for use by the lessee), the lessee recognises (*IFRS 16: para. 22*):

- A lease liability
- A right-of-use asset

#### 1.3.2 Lease liability

The lease liability is initially measured at the **present value of future lease payments, which are those lease payments not paid on or before the commencement date**, discounted at the **interest rate implicit in the lease** (or the lessee's incremental borrowing rate\* if not readily determinable) (*IFRS 16: para. 26*).

\* The rate to borrow over a similar term, with similar security, to obtain an asset of similar value in a similar economic environment (*IFRS 16: Appendix A*)

The lease liability cash flows to be discounted include the following (*IFRS 16: para. 27*):

- Fixed payments, less any lease incentives receivable
- Variable payments that depend on an index (eg CPI) or rate (eg market rent)
- Amounts expected to be payable under residual value guarantees (eg where a lessee guarantees to the lessor that an asset will be worth a specified amount at the end of the lease)
- Purchase options (if reasonably certain to be exercised).

Other variable payments (eg payments that arise due to level of use of the asset) are accounted for as period costs in profit or loss as incurred (*IFRS 16: para. 38*).

The lease liability is **subsequently measured** by (*IFRS 16: para. 36*):

- Increasing it by interest on the lease liability (also recognised as an expense in the financing category in profit or loss)
- Reducing it by lease payments made.

#### 1.3.3 Right-of-use asset

The right-of-use asset is initially measured at its **cost** (*IFRS 16: para. 23*), which includes (*IFRS 16: para. 24*):

- The amount of the **initial** measurement of the lease liability (the present value of lease payments **not** paid on or before the commencement date)
- Payments made at/before the lease commencement date (less any lease incentives received)
- Initial direct costs (eg legal costs) incurred by the lessee
- An estimate of dismantling and restoration costs (where an obligation exists).

The right-of-use asset is normally measured subsequently at **cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment losses** in accordance with the cost model of IAS 16 *Property, Plant and Equipment* (*IFRS 16: para. 29*).

The right-of-use asset is **depreciated** from the commencement date to the **earlier of** the **end of its useful life** or **end of the lease term** (end of its useful life if ownership is expected to be transferred) (*IFRS 16: paras. 31–32*). The depreciation is recognised as an expense in the operating category of the statement of profit or loss (unless it qualifies to be included as part of another asset).

Alternatively the right-of-use asset is accounted for in accordance with:

1. The **revaluation model of IAS 16** (**optional** where the right-of-use asset relates to a class of property, plant and equipment measured under the revaluation model, and where elected, must apply to all right-of-use assets relating to that class) (*IFRS 16: para. 35*)
2. The **fair value model of IAS 40** ***Investment Property*** (compulsory if the right-of-use asset meets the definition of investment property and the lessee uses the fair value model for its investment property) (IFRS 16: para. 34)

Right-of-use assets are presented either as a separate line item in the statement of financial position **or** by disclosing which line items include right-of-use assets (*IFRS 16: para. 47*).

#### 1.3.4 Optional recognition exemptions

IFRS 16 provides an optional exemption from the full requirements of the standard for (*IFRS 16: para. 5*):

- **Short-term leases** (leases with a lease term of 12 months or fewer) (*IFRS 16: Appendix A*)
- Leases for which the **underlying asset is low value** (eg tablet and personal computers, small items of office furniture and telephones) (*IFRS 16: para. B8*)

If the entity elects to take the exemption, lease payments are **recognised as an expense** (operating category) on a **straight-line basis over the lease term** or another systematic basis (if more representative of the pattern of the lessee's benefits) (*IFRS 16: para. 6*).

The assessment of whether an underlying asset is of low value is performed on an **absolute basis** based on the value if the asset when it is new. It is not a question of materiality: different lessees should come to the same conclusion about whether assets are low value, regardless of the entity's size (*IFRS 16: para. B4*).

#### Illustration 3: Recognition exemptions I

Inkey Co leases a second-hand car which has a market value of $2,000. When new it would have cost $15,000. Inkey Co’s directors believe they can apply the recognition exemption in IFRS 16 because the car is of low value.

**Required**

Explain whether Inkey Co’s directors are correct.

**Solution**

Inkey Co’s directors are not correct. The lease would not qualify as a lease of a low-value asset because the car would not have been low value when new. Inkey Co cannot apply the recognition exemption in IFRS 16 to this lease.

#### Illustration 4: Recognition exemptions II

Inkey Co has a year end of 30 June 20X6. On 1 May 20X6, Inkey Co made a payment of $64,000 for an eight-month lease of a recycling machine. Inkey Co elected to use the optional recognition exemptions in IFRS 16 as the lease is for less than 12 months.

**Required**

Show how the lease should be accounted for in the year to 30 June 20X6.

**Solution**

To correctly account for the lease in the year to 30 June 20X6, Inkey Co should charge $16,000 ($64,000 × 2/8 months) to profit or loss (operating category). $48,000 would be presented as a prepayment within current assets in the statement of financial position.

#### 1.3.5 Remeasurement

The lease liability is **remeasured** (if necessary) for any reassessment of amounts payable (*IFRS 16: para. 39*).

The revised lease payments are discounted using the **original discount interest** **rate** where the change relates to an expected payment on a **residual value guarantee** or **payments linked to an index or rate** (and a **revised discount rate** where there is a **change in lease term, purchase option** or **payments linked to a floating interest rate**) (*IFRS 16: paras. 40–43*).

The change in the lease liability is recognised as an **adjustment to the right-of-use asset** (or in profit or loss if the right-of-use asset is reduced to zero) (*IFRS 16: para. 39*).

Example: Revision of lease term

*(Adapted from IFRS 16 Illustrative Example 13)*

Lester enters into a ten-year lease of a floor of a building, with an option to extend for five years. Lease payments are $50,000 per year during the initial term and $55,000 per year during the optional period, all payable at the beginning of each year. The interest rate implicit in the lease was not readily determinable. Lester's incremental borrowing rate was 5% per annum.

Lester is now in the sixth year of the ten-year lease, with its option to renew for another five years. The optional period has not been included in the initial assessment of the lease term. Lester acquires Wester, which has been leasing a floor in another building. The lease entered into by Wester contains a termination option that is exercisable by Wester. Following the acquisition of Wester, Lester needs two floors in a building suitable for the increased workforce of the combined companies. To minimise costs, Lester (a) enters into a separate eight-year lease of another floor in the building it currently occupies that will be available for use at the end of Year 7 and (b) terminates early the lease entered into by Wester with effect from the beginning of Year 8. Wester will then move into the new floor leased by Lester.

Lester's incremental borrowing rate at the end of Year 6 is 6% per annum.

Moving Wester's staff to the same building occupied by Lester creates an economic incentive for Lester to extend its original lease at the end of the non-cancellable period of ten years. The acquisition of Wester and the relocation of Wester's staff is a significant event that is within the control of Lester and affects whether Lester is reasonably certain to exercise the extension option not previously included in its determination of the lease term. This is because the original floor has greater utility (and thus provides greater benefits) to Lester than alternative assets that could be leased for a similar amount to the lease payments for the optional period – Lester would incur additional costs if it were to lease a similar floor in a different building because the workforce would be located in different buildings. Consequently, at the end of Year 6, Lester concludes that it is now reasonably certain to exercise the option to extend its original lease as a result of its acquisition and planned relocation of Wester.

Lester remeasures the lease liability at the present value of four payments of $50,000 followed by five payments of $55,000, all discounted at the revised discount rate of 6% per annum.

Lassie plc leased an item of equipment on the following terms:

| Lease term: | 5 years |
| Annual lease payments (commencing 1 January 20X1): | $200,000 (rising annually by CPI as at 31 December) |
| Interest rate implicit in the lease: | 6.2% |

The present value of future lease payments **not paid** at 1 January 20X1 was $690,000. The price to purchase the asset outright would have been $1,200,000.

Inflation measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for the year ending 31 December 20X1 was 2%. As a result the lease payments commencing 1 January 20X2 rose to $204,000. The present value of lease payments for the remaining four years of the lease becomes approximately $747,300 using the original discount rate of 6.2%.

**Required**

Discuss how Lassie plc should account for the lease and remeasurement in the year ended 31 December 20X1.

**Solution**

On the commencement date, Lassie plc recognises a lease liability of $690,000 for the present value of lease payments **not** paid at the 1 January 20X1 commencement date.

A right-of-use asset of $890,000 is recognised comprising the amount initially recognised as the lease liability $690,000 plus $200,000 payment made on the commencement date.

The right-of-use asset is depreciated over five years. Its carrying amount at 31 December 20X1 (before adjustment for reassessment of the lease liability is $712,000 ($890,000 – ($890,000/5 years)).

The carrying amount of the lease liability at the end of the first year (before reassessment of the lease liability) is $732,780 (Working). On that date, the future lease payments are revised by 2%. The lease liability is therefore revised to $747,300.

The difference of $14,520 adjusts the carrying amount of the right-of-use asset, increasing it to $726,520. This will be depreciated over the remaining useful life of the asset of four years from 20X2.

### 1.4 Separating multiple components of a lease contract

A contract may contain both a lease component and a non-lease component. In other words, it may include an amount payable by the lessee for activities and costs that do not transfer goods or services to the lessee (*IFRS 16: para. B33*). These activities and costs might, for example, include maintenance, repairs or cleaning.

IFRS 16 requires entities to **account for the lease component of the contract separately from the non-lease component**. The entity must split the rental or lease payment and:

- Account for the lease component under IFRS 16; and
- Account for the service element separately, generally as an expense in profit or loss (operating category).

The consideration in the contract is **allocated on the basis of the stand-alone prices** of the lease component(s) and the non-lease component(s).

#### Illustration 5: Separating multiple components of a lease contract

Livery Co leases a delivery van from Bettalease Co for three years at $12,000 per year. This payment includes servicing costs.

Livery Co could lease the same make and model of van for $11,000 per year and would need to pay $2,000 a year for servicing.

**Required**

Show the allocation of the lease across the components.

**Solution**

Livery Co would allocate $10,154 ($12,000 × $11,000 ÷ $(11,000 + 2,000)) to the lease component and account for that as a lease under IFRS 16.

Livery Co would allocate $1,846 ($12,000 × $2,000 ÷ $(11,000 + 2,000)) to the servicing component and recognise it in profit or loss as an expense (operating category).

### 1.5 Deferred tax implications

[Embedded video](https://bpp.cloud.panopto.eu/Panopto/Pages/Embed.aspx?id=955c8635-e4c8-4a20-80b4-b10200c50381&autoplay=false&offerviewer=true&showtitle=true&showbrand=true&captions=true&interactivity=all)

Under a lease, the lessee recognises a right-of-use asset and a corresponding lease liability. The carrying amount of the right-of-use asset and of the lease liability should be compared to their individual tax bases. If temporary differences arise, then the related deferred tax liability and/or deferred tax asset should be recognised.

The tax base will depend on the tax laws of the jurisdiction, which you will be given in an exam question.

#### Illustration 6: Deferred tax on leases

On 1 January 20X1, Calston Co entered into a contract to lease a building for 10 years. On commencement of the lease, Calston Co recognised a lease liability of $100,000, being the present value of future lease payments, and a right-of-use asset of $110,000, being the initial lease liability plus $10,000 of legal costs incurred. Lease payments of $15,000 are paid annually in arrears. The interest rate implicit in the lease is 7.5% and the right-of-use asset is depreciated over 10 years on a straight-line basis.

Local tax rules allow tax deductions for lease payments and initial direct costs when they are paid. The tax rate is 20%. Calston Co has determined that it can offset any resulting deferred tax assets and liabilities.

**Required**

Explain the deferred tax implications of this transaction at 1 January 20X1 and the reporting date of 31 December 20X1.

**Solution**

**At 1 January 20X1**

1. Lease liabilityThe carrying amount of the lease liability is $100,000. The tax base of the lease liability is $0 because it is the carrying amount of the lease liability less the future tax deductions of $100,000 for the lease payments. A deductible temporary difference of $100,000 arises, and therefore a deferred tax asset of $20,000 ($100,000 × 20%).

1. Right-of-use assetThe right-of-use asset has a carrying amount of $110,000, which is made up of:
    i. $100,000 of the initial lease liability. The tax base is $0 because the tax deduction relates to the lease liability and no tax deduction will be available for the asset.
    ii. $10,000 of initial direct costs (legal fees). The tax base of the legal fees is $0 because the tax deduction has already been applied when Calston Co made the payment.

Therefore, the right-of-use asset has a tax base of $0. A taxable temporary difference of $110,000 ($100,000 + $10,000) arises and a resulting deferred tax liability of $22,000 ($110,000 × 20%).

The deferred tax asset and liability can be offset, so Calston Co should recognise deferred tax at 1 January 20X1 as follows:

1. Lease liability

1. Right-of-use asset The right-of-use asset is depreciated over 10 years on a straight-line basis. Its carrying amount at 31 December 20X1 is therefore $99,000 ($110,000 × 9/10). #### Activity 2: Deferred tax

On 1 January 20X3, Heggie Co (Heggie) leased a machine under a five-year lease. The useful life of the asset to Heggie was four years and there is no residual value.

The annual lease payments are $6 million payable in arrears each year on 31 December. The present value of the future lease payments not paid on or before commencement was $24 million using the interest rate implicit in the lease of approximately 8% per annum. At the end of the lease term legal title remains with the lessor. Heggie incurred $0.4 million of direct costs of setting up the lease.

The directors have not leased an asset before and are unsure how to account for it and whether there are any deferred tax implications.

The company can claim a tax deduction for the annual lease payments and lease set-up costs, and deferred tax assets and deferred tax liabilities may be offset. Assume a tax rate of 20%.

**Required**

Discuss, with suitable computations, the accounting treatment of the above transaction in Heggie’s accounting records at 1 January 20X3 and in Heggie’s financial statements as at 31 December 20X3. Work to the nearest $0.1 million.

**Solution**

Lease accounting

A right-of-use asset of $24.4 million should be recognised at 1 January 20X3. This comprises the $24 million present value of lease payments **not** paid at the 1 January 20X3 commencement date plus the 'initial direct costs' incurred in setting up the lease of $0.4 million.

The asset should be depreciated from the commencement date (1 January 20X3) to the earlier of the end of the asset's useful life (4 years) and the end of the lease term (5 years) unless the legal title reverts to the lessee at the end of the lease term. Here, as the legal title remains with the lessor, the asset should be depreciated over four years, giving an annual depreciation charge of $6.1 million to be recognised in the operating category in profit or loss ($24.4 million/4 years) and a carrying amount of $18.3 million at 31 December 20X3.

A lease liability should initially be recognised on 1 January 20X3 at the present value of lease payments **not** paid at the commencement date. This amounts to $24 million. An annual finance cost of 8% of the carrying amount should be recognised in profit or loss (financing category) and added to the liability. The first lease instalment on 31 December 20X3 is then deducted from the liability, giving a carrying amount of $19.9 million (Working) at 31 December 20X3.

Deferred tax

*Lease liability*

For the lease liability, tax relief is granted as lease payments are made. The tax base of the lease liability is zero because it is determined as the carrying amount of the lease liability of $24 million less the future tax deductions, which are also $24 million. On 1 January 20X3, this gives rise to a deductible temporary difference of $24 million and a deferred tax asset of $4.8 million. At 31 December 20X3 the carrying amount has decreased to $19.9 million and the tax base is zero. This gives rise to a deductible temporary difference of $19.9 million and a deferred tax asset of $4.0 million ($19.9 million × 20%).

*Right-of-use asset*

The right-of-use asset is the sum of:

## 2 Lessor accounting

### 2.1 Impact of IFRS 18 *Presentation and Disclosure in Financial Statements*

As set out in Chapter 1, an entity is required to categorise and present income and expenses within one of three main categories in the statement of profit or loss – namely operating, investing and financing. The operating category is based on an entity’s main business activities whereas the investing category includes income and expenses from investments made individually (or separately) and largely independently of the main business activities.

An entity that is a lessor will need to consider whether investing in assets such as investment properties and investments in leases is a specified main business activity or not. If the entity considers these activities to be a specified main business activity, income and expenses arising from the activities will be included in the operating category. If this is not considered to be a specified main business activity, income and expenses will be included in the investing category (IFRS 18: para. 49).

### 2.2 Classification of leases for lessor accounting

The approach to lessor accounting classifies leases into two types (*IFRS 16: para. 61*):

- **Finance leases** (where a lease receivable is recognised in the statement of financial position); and
- **Operating leases** (which are accounted for as rental income).

> **Key terms**
>
> **Finance lease:**
> A lease that **transfers substantially all the risks and rewards** incidental to ownership of an underlying asset.
>
> **Operating lease:**
> A lease that **does not transfer** substantially all the risks and rewards incidental to ownership of an underlying asset. *(IFRS 16: Appendix A)*
>

IFRS 16 identifies five examples of situations which **would normally** lead to a lease being classified as a finance lease (*IFRS 16: para. 63*):

1. The lease **transfers ownership** of the underlying asset to the lessee by the end of the lease term.
2. The lessee has the **option to purchase** the underlying asset at a price expected to be **sufficiently lower than fair value** at the exercise date, so that it is reasonably certain, at the inception date, that the option will be exercised.
3. The lease term is for a **major part of the economic life** of the underlying asset even if title is not transferred.
4. The present value of the lease payments at the inception date amounts to at least **substantially all** of the fair value of the underlying asset.
5. The underlying asset is of such **specialised** nature that only the lessee can use it without major modifications.

Additionally, the following situations which **could** lead to a lease being classified as a finance lease (*IFRS 16: para. 64*):

1. Any **losses on cancellation** are borne by the lessee.
2. Gains/losses on changes in **residual value** accrue to the lessee.
3. The lessee can **continue to lease for a secondary term** at a rent substantially lower than market rent.

### 2.3 Finance leases

#### 2.3.1 Recognition and measurement

At the commencement date (the date the lessor makes the underlying asset available for use by the lessee), the lessor (*IFRS 16: para. 67*):

- Derecognises the underlying asset; and
- Recognises a lease receivable at an amount equal to the **net investment in the lease**.

Any difference between the carrying amount of the underlying asset and the net investment in the lease is recognised in profit or loss. If this is considered to be a specified main business activity this would be categorised in the operating category. If not, this is classified in the investing category.

The **net investment in the lease** (*IFRS 16: Appendix A*) is the sum of:

| Present value of lease payments receivable by the lessor | X |
| --- | --- |
| Present value of any unguaranteed residual value accruing to the lessor | <u>X</u> |
| Net investment in the lease | <u>X</u> |

An unguaranteed residual value arises where a lessor expects to be able to sell an asset at the end of the lease term for more than any minimum amount guaranteed by the lessee in the lease contract. Amounts guaranteed by the lessee are included in the 'present value of lease payments receivable by the lessor' as they will always be received, so only the unguaranteed amount needs to be added on, which accrues to the lessor because it owns the underlying asset.

**Finance income** is recognised over the lease term based on a pattern reflecting a constant periodic rate of return on the lessor's net investment in the lease (*IFRS 16: para. 75*), either in the operating category or the investing category of profit or loss depending on whether or not this is a specified main business activity.

The **derecognition** and **impairment** requirements of IFRS 9 *Financial Instruments* are applied to the net investment in the lease (*IFRS 16: para. 77*).

### 2.4 Operating leases

#### 2.4.1 Recognition and measurement

Lease rentals received under operating leases are recognised as income on either a **straight-line basis or another systematic basis** (if more representative of the pattern in which benefit from use of the underlying asset is diminished) (*IFRS 16: para. 81*). If the rental of assets under operating leases is considered to be a specified main business activity (for example a vehicle rental business or a property rental business) this would be included in the operating category in the statement of profit or loss. If not, this income would be part of the investing category.

Any initial direct costs incurred in obtaining the lease are added to the carrying amount of the underlying asset. IAS 16 *Property, Plant and Equipment* or IAS 38 *Intangible Assets* then applies to the depreciation or amortisation of the underlying asset as appropriate (*IFRS 16: paras. 83–84*).

#### Illustration 9: Lessor – operating lease

A lessor leases a property to a lessee under an operating lease for five years at an annual rate of $100,000. However, the contract states that the first six months are 'rent-free'. This is not a specified main business activity.

**Solution**

The benefit received from the asset is earned over the five years. However, in the first year, the lessor only receives $100,000 × 6/12 = $50,000. Lease rentals of $450,000 ($50,000 + ($100,000 × 4 years)) are received over the five year lease term.

Therefore, the lessor recognises income of $90,000 per year ($450,000/5 years) in the investing category of the statement of profit or loss.

A receivable of $40,000 is recognised at the end of Year 1 ($90,000 – $50,000 cash received).

### 2.5 Subleases

A sublease is the re-leasing of the underlying asset by a lessee to a third party, while the original lease between the original lessor and lessee (the ‘head lease’) remains in effect (*IFRS 16: Appendix A*).

![Flowchart depicting a leasing arrangement where the head lessor (legal owner of asset) enters into a head lease (Contract 1) with a lessee. The lessee recognises lease liability and right-of-use asset. The lessee then acts as an intermediate lessor, recognizing either an operating lease or a finance lease, and subleases the asset (Contract 2) to another lessee.](media/images/22673_0.1.jpg)

As well as being a lessee, the lessee is now the **intermediate lessor** and should determine whether the sublease is a **finance lease** or an **operating lease** (*IFRS 16: para. B58*). This is done using the same criteria used by all other lessors (see section 2.1), except with reference to the right-of-use asset, and not the underlying asset (eg the item of PPE). Once the classification is determined, the accounting is the same as for any other finance lease or operating lease.

#### Illustration 10: Sublease classified as a finance lease

On 1 January 20X5 Aurora Co, the lessee, entered into a lease agreement with Lessor X for the right to use a building. The lease term is five years with lease payments payable at the end of each year. Aurora Co correctly recognised a right-of-use asset and lease liability in relation to this lease.

On 1 January 20X7, Aurora Co entered into a contract to sublease the building to Borealis Co for three years, with lease payments payable at the end of each year. Borealis Co is responsible for insuring the building for the duration of its lease. The sub-leasing of buildings is not a specified main business activity of Aurora Co.

**Required**

Explain how Aurora Co should account for the subleasing arrangement under IFRS 16.

**Solution**

On 1 January 20X5, Aurora Co correctly recognised a right-of-use asset and a lease liability relating to the head lease.

On 1 January 20X7, Aurora Co leased the building to Borealis Co. Aurora Co is therefore an intermediate lessor in this arrangement and should determine whether the lease is a finance lease or an operating lease.

The fact that the sublease is for the whole of the remaining useful life of the right-of-use asset and that Borealis Co is responsible for the insurance indicates that this is a finance lease.

Aurora Co should:

1. Derecognise the right-of-use asset (relating to the head lease) that it transfers to Borealis Co
2. Recognise the net investment in the sublease
3. Recognise any difference between the carrying amount of the right-of-use asset and the net investment in the sublease in profit or loss in the investing category
4. Continue to recognise the lease liability relating to the head lease in the statement of financial position, as this represents the lease payments owed to the head lessor

In the statement of profit or loss for the duration of the sublease, Aurora Co will recognise finance income in relation to the sublease (in the investing category) as well as an interest expense in relation to the head lease (in the financing category).

#### Illustration 11: Sublease classified as an operating lease

On 1 January 20X1 Amber Co, the lessee, entered into a lease agreement with Lessor Y for the right to use a building. The lease term is five years with lease payments payable at the end of each year. Amber Co correctly recognised a right-of-use asset and lease liability in relation to this lease.

On 1 January 20X1, the commencement date of the head lease, Amber Co entered into a contract to sublease the building to Spyglass Co for two years, with lease payments payable at the end of each year. Amber Co is responsible for insuring the building for the duration of the sublease. Amber Co is a property rental company, and this type of transaction is considered to be a specified main business activity of Amber Co.

**Required**

Explain how Amber Co should account for the subleasing arrangement under IFRS 16.

**Solution**

On 1 January 20X1, Amber Co correctly recognised a right-of-use asset and a lease liability relating to the head lease.

On 1 January 20X1, Amber Co leased the building to Spyglass Co. Amber Co is therefore an intermediate lessor in this arrangement and should determine whether the lease is a finance lease or an operating lease.

The sublease is only for two of the remaining five years of the useful life of the right-of-use asset arising from the head lease, and Amber Co retains responsibility for insuring the building. This indicates that the risks and rewards associated with the right-of-use asset remain with Amber Co and this is an operating lease.

Amber Co should retain the right-of-use asset and lease liability relating to the head lease in its statement of financial position, and the associated depreciation and finance costs should be charged to Amber Co’s profit or loss for the duration of the head lease term. The depreciation will be charged to the operating category and the finance cost to the financing category.

Amber Co should recognise rental income in relation to the operating lease on a straight-line basis for the duration of the sublease in the operating category of the statement of profit or loss.

**Note.** The right-of-use asset held by Amber Co in relation to the building meets the definition of an investment property under IAS 40 *Investment Properties*, as it is held to earn rentals (*IAS 40: para. 5*).

#### 2.5.1 Exceptions related to the lessee recognition exemption

There are two exceptions in relation to subleases and the recognition exemptions for lessees:

1. If the lessee of an asset subleases or expects to sublease that asset, then it cannot apply the recognition exemption for leases of low-value assets (*IFRS 16: para B7*).
2. In a subleasing arrangement, if the head lease is a short-term lease that the entity as a lessee has accounted for by applying the recognition exemptions for short-term leases, then the sublease is always classified as an operating lease and cannot be a finance lease (*IFRS 16: para B58*).

## 3 Sale and leaseback transactions

[Embedded video](https://bpp.cloud.panopto.eu/Panopto/Pages/Embed.aspx?id=f618ad52-2eab-42a4-a825-b10200e81ff3&autoplay=false&offerviewer=true&showtitle=true&showbrand=true&captions=true&interactivity=all)

A sale and leaseback transaction arises where an entity (the seller-lessee) transfers ('sells') an asset to another entity (the buyer-lessor) and then leases it back.

The entity applies the requirements of IFRS 15 *Revenue from Contracts with Customers* to **determine whether in substance a sale occurs** (ie whether a performance obligation is satisfied and control of the asset passes to the buyer-lessor) (*IFRS 16: para. 99*).

### 3.1 Transfer of the asset is in substance a sale

#### 3.1.1 Seller-lessee

As a sale has occurred, the seller-lessee should **derecognise the carrying amount of the asset**, and **recognise**:

- A **right-of-use asset** measured at the proportion of the previous carrying amount that relates to the right of use retained (*IFRS 16: para. 100*). This is calculated as:Right-of-use asset = carrying amount of asset × (present value of future lease payments (PVFLP)/fair value of asset (FV))
- A **gain/loss** in relation to the rights transferred to the buyer-lessor (*IFRS 16: para. 100*). This is calculated in three stages:Stage 1: calculate the total gain = fair value – carrying amount Stage 2: calculate the gain relating to the rights retained = total gain × PVFLP/FVStage 3: calculate the gain relating to the rights transferred = total gain – gain relating to rights retained The gain or loss will be recognised in the statement of profit or loss category that reflects how the underlying asset was used. If the asset was used in the main business activities of the entity the gain or loss will be included in the operating category. If the asset was an investment made separately and largely independently of the main business activities, the gain or loss will be included in the investing category. If this is considered to be a material non-recurring item it will need to be presented separately.
- A **lease liability** measured at the present value of the future lease payments (adjusted if sale proceeds are more than fair value)

Sale proceeds not equal to fair value

If the **sales proceeds** **do not equal the asset's fair value** the following adjustments are made (*IFRS 16: para. 101*):

1. **Sales proceeds are less than asset’s fair value** The difference between sales proceeds and fair value is accounted for as a **prepayment** of lease payments and so is **added to the right-of-use asset** as per the normal IFRS 16 treatment for initial measurement of a right-of-use asset.
2. Sales proceeds are more than asset’s fair value The difference between sales proceeds and fair value is treated as **additional financing** provided by the buyer-lessor to the seller-lessee and is recognised as a financial liability.

#### 3.1.2 Buyer-lessor

The **buyer-lessor** accounts for the purchase as a **normal purchase** and for the lease in accordance with IFRS 16 (*IFRS 16: para. 100*).

| 120,000/1.0455 | <u>96,294</u> |
|  | <u>526,797</u> |

Of this, $476,797 relates to the lease and $50,000 relates to the additional financing. The annual payment of $120,000 needs to be allocated into the amount relating to the lease (five annual payments of $108,610) and the amount relating to the additional financing (five annual payments of $11,390).

At the commencement date, the seller-lessee measures the right-of-use asset arising from the leaseback of the building at the proportion of the previous carrying amount of the building that relates to the right of use retained. This is calculated as:

**Carrying amount × present value of future lease payments (lease element only)/fair value**

In this example: $600,000 × $476,797/$750,000 = $381,437

Selleasy Co only recognises the amount of gain that relates to the rights transferred. The gain on sale of the building is $150,000 (750,000 – 600,000), of which:

1. $150,000 × $476,797/$750,000 = $95,360 – relates to the rights retained
2. The balance: $150,000 – $95,360 = $54,640 – relates to the rights transferred to the buyer.

At the commencement date the lessee accounts for the transaction as follows:

|  |  | $ | $ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| DR | Cash | 800,000 |  |
| DR | Right-of-use asset | 381,437 |  |
| CR | Building |  | 600,000 |
| CR | Lease liability |  | 476,797 |
| CR | Financial liability |  | 50,000 |
| CR | Gain on rights transferred (operating category) |  | 54,640 |

The right-of-use asset will be depreciated over five years and the gain will be recognised in the operating category of the statement of profit or loss. The lease liability will be increased each year by interest at 4.5% and reduced by the lease payments of $108,610. The financial liability will be increased each year by interest at 4.5% and reduced by the interest payments of $11,390.

### 3.2 Transfer of the asset is NOT in substance a sale

#### 3.2.1 Seller-lessee

The seller-lessee continues to recognise the transferred asset and recognises a financial liability equal to the transfer proceeds (and accounts for it in accordance with IFRS 9) (*IFRS 16: para. 103*).

#### 3.2.2 Buyer-lessor

The **buyer-lessor** does not recognise the transferred asset and **recognises a financial asset** equal to the transfer proceeds (and accounts for it in accordance with IFRS 9) (*IFRS 16: para. 103*).

## Chapter summary

![This is a visual summary of the topics covered in this chapter.](media/images/22679_1.0.jpg)

### Knowledge diagnostic

#### 1.

#### Lessee accounting

Where a **contract contains a lease**, a **right-of-use asset** and a **liability for the present value of lease payments not paid on or before the commencement date** are recognised in the lessee's books.

An **optional exemption** is available for **short-term leases** (lease term of 12 months or less) and leases of **low value assets**, which can be accounted for as an **expense** over the lease term.

**Deferred tax** should be calculated when temporary differences arise on the lease liability and right-of-use asset. The tax base will depend on the tax rules, but it is often the case that lease payments are tax deductible when paid and tax deductions are given for initial direct costs when paid. Deferred tax assets and deferred tax liabilities should be recognised separately for the lease liability and right-of-use asset, but may be offset where this is permitted by tax law in the jurisdiction.

#### 2.

#### Lessor accounting

Assets leased out under **finance leases** are **derecognised** from the lessor's books and **replaced with a receivable**, the **'net investment in the lease'**.

Assets leased under an **operating lease** remain in the lessor's books and **rental income** is recognised on a **straight line basis** (or another systematic basis if more representative of the pattern in which benefit from the underlying asset is diminished).

Sub-leases are treated in the same way as other leases using the operating lease/finance lease model, but with reference to the right-of-use asset rather than the underlying asset.

#### 3.

#### Sale and leaseback transactions

Accounting for sale and leaseback transactions depends on whether in **substance** a sale has occurred (ie a performance obligation is satisfied) in accordance with IFRS 15 *Revenue from Contracts with Customers*.

Where the **transfer is in substance a sale**, the **seller-lessee** **derecognises the asset** sold, and **recognises a right-of-use asset and lease liability** relating to the right of use retained and a **gain/loss** in relation to the rights transferred. The **buyer-lessor** accounts for the transaction as a **normal purchase** and a lease.

Where the **transfer is in substance** ***not*** **a sale**, the **seller-lessee** accounts for the **proceeds** as a **financial liability** (in accordance with IFRS 9). The **buyer-lessor** recognises a **financial asset**.
